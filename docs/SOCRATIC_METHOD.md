# How to co-train your child classically, alongside Bede

*For parents. This is the "what is Bede actually doing, and how do I join in"
guide — read it once, then keep the [example dialogues](#borrow-the-method-yourself-example-dialogues-by-stage)
handy for the dinner table.*

Bede never hands you the whole job. [Bede's constitution](CONSTITUTION.md) is
explicit about this: *"The parent remains the primary educator and final
human approver."* Bede's own sacred rules go further — rule 15 states
plainly that **the parent is the curriculum director**; Bede implements your
plan and never overrides your judgment. This guide is about the part of that
partnership that's easy to miss: not just *setting up* Bede's lessons, but
*joining in* the actual method, so your child hears the same kind of question
from you at the dinner table that they hear from Bede at the tablet.

## What "Socratic" actually means here

Not a vague label — a specific, repeatable loop that governs every one of
Bede's turns, straight from the constitution:

> **Wonder → Question → Define → Observe → Examine Evidence and Sources →
> Reason → Test with Counterexamples → Judge Truthfully → Express or Act →
> Reflect and Return to Wonder**

In practice, that collapses to one hard rule you'll see in every session:
**Bede never gives the answer directly.** It asks the question that makes
your child think, listens to *exactly* what they actually said — not what it
expected — and asks the next question that meets them right where that
answer left them. As Bede's own instructions put it: *"A child who discovers
a truth owns it; a child who is told a truth only borrows it."*

That's the whole method. Everything else in this guide is about doing that
same thing yourself, unplugged, with a real book in your hands.

## Why this needs you, not just the tablet

A tutor who only ever appears on a screen teaches a child that thinking
happens *at* the tablet. A parent who asks the same kind of question over
the dinner table, on a walk, or mid-chapter of a book you're reading aloud
together teaches something truer: that thinking is just what your family
does. Three concrete ways to close that loop:

- **Read the same material first.** Whatever you put in a student's
  `current_unit` or `lesson_focus` note (Parent Setup) is exactly what Bede
  anchors its questions to — if you've read the same chapter, you can ask
  the same kind of question yourself before or after the session, not just
  set the topic and step away.
- **Ask your child to narrate the *questions*, not just the answers.** After
  a session, "What did Bede ask you today?" often surfaces more than "What
  did you learn?" — narration (telling a thing back in your own words) is
  the classical exercise Bede leans on constantly, and it works exactly the
  same way when the audience is you instead of Bede.
- **Pick the starting point that matches how involved you want to be.**
  Parent Setup's companion-mode picker — **Book Companion**, **A Bit More
  Structure**, or **Full Daily Plan** — isn't just a subject-list shortcut.
  Book Companion nudges Bede to explicitly anchor its questions on whatever
  book your family is already reading together, rather than driving its own
  agenda — the right starting point if you intend to read alongside your
  child and want Bede visibly deferring to that material.

## The method changes shape by age — so should yours

Bede asks differently depending on which stage of the trivium your child is
in (set by grade in Parent Setup). Matching your own questions to the same
stage is the single most useful thing in this guide:

| Stage | Grades | What the child is ready for | What *you* should ask |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Grammar** | K–2 | Gathering language, images, and facts — "receiving as a hungry guest at a feast, not a critic at a desk." Short attention, oral only. | Wonder-sized, concrete questions. "What did you notice?" "What happened first?" Never a written assignment at this age — narration is spoken, playful, brief. |
| **Logic** | 3–5 | Reasoning about what's been gathered — cause and effect, "why," categorizing, forming a first opinion with a reason behind it. | "Why do you think that happened?" "What would happen if...?" "How is this like/unlike the other one?" Written narration starts here, but stays occasional, not the norm. |
| **Rhetoric** | 6–8 | Debate, persuasive argument, nuance, defending a claim against an opposing view. | "What's the strongest argument *against* your position?" "How would you convince someone who disagreed?" Writing becomes the norm, not the exception. |

## What not to do (Bede doesn't do it either)

- **Don't quiz.** A question with only one acceptable answer, asked to check
  a box, isn't Socratic — it's a test wearing a question mark. Bede's own
  narration checks are explicitly *never* a quiz; keep yours the same way.
- **Don't rescue with the answer.** The hardest habit to break, especially
  mid-frustration. When your child is stuck, Bede's rule is to slow down and
  offer a gentler, more concrete version of the *same* question — an easier
  rung of the same ladder — never the answer itself.
- **Don't treat "wrong" as a stopping point.** A confused or half-right
  answer is where the next good question lives, not a failure to correct.
- **Don't turn every conversation into a lesson.** Bede weaves faith and
  wonder in *naturally*, never forced into every exchange — the same
  restraint serves you well at the dinner table.

## Borrow the method yourself: example dialogues by stage

These aren't scripts to read verbatim — they're the *shape* of a good
question at each stage, the same shape Bede reaches for.

**Grammar stage (K–2)**, after reading a picture book about a garden:
> *"What was your favorite thing that grew in the garden?"* → child answers
> → *"What do you think that plant needed to grow — what did you see the
> gardener give it?"* → child answers → *"If nobody watered it, what do you
> think would happen?"*

**Logic stage (3–5)**, after a history chapter about a famous decision:
> *"Why do you think he made that choice, and not the other one?"* → child
> answers → *"What do you think he was afraid would happen if he'd chosen
> differently?"* → child answers → *"Was there anything he could have done
> that we haven't talked about yet?"*

**Rhetoric stage (6–8)**, after a passage presenting an argument:
> *"What's the author's strongest piece of evidence?"* → child answers →
> *"Now argue the other side — what's the best case AGAINST this?"* → child
> answers → *"Having heard both, where do you actually land, and why?"*

In every case: ask, then genuinely wait. Let the pause sit. The question
that comes next should be built from what your child just said, not the
question you'd already decided to ask regardless.

## Checking in without turning it into a report card

The [Progress page](PARENT_SETUP.md) is worth a look every so often, but
read it the way Bede reads a narration: as a picture forming, not a grade.
A "developing" mastery level or an early-stage learner profile isn't a
problem to fix — it's exactly where your child actually is right now, and
that's the only honest place to start the next question from.
